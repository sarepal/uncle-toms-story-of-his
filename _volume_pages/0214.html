{% raw %}" SUMMARY OF ' UNCLE TOM'S' PUBLIC{% endraw %}
{% raw %}SERVICES.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In August, 1876, on Sunday, at Victoria Park Tabernacle, to a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}congregation exceeding 2,000 persons.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}At Wood Green, on Thursday, in the "Tent" erected for evan¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}gelistic services by T. B. Smithies, Esq., editor of the "British{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Workman." In the unavoidable absence of Baroness Coutts, she{% endraw %}
{% raw %}kindly deputed Rev. Mr. Sinclair to take her carriage and place it{% endraw %}
{% raw %}at " Uncle Tom's" service at the close of the proceedings.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}In September, 1876, again at Wood Green, in the Wesleyan{% endraw %}
{% raw %}chapel, which accommodated over 1,000 persons.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Little Wild Street Baptist Chapel, Drury Laiie.—Crowded beyond{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the capacity of the place.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Epsom.—This neighbourhood has been rarely moved with an{% endraw %}
{% raw %}excitement like that which followed "Uncle Tom's" visit here.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Brixton.—On Friday, at the Congregational Church, to a large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and appreciative audience.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mildmay Park.—On Sunday, in this large and beautiful Con¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}ference Hall; although it seats 2,500 persons, the building was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}crowded in every part, and hundreds failed to gain admission.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Stoke Newinejton.—On Sunday evening, in the Congregational{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Church, Walford Road.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Wallington.—On Wednesday the place of meeting was thronged.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Rev. Dr. Whittemore, and Rev. J. Williams, of the vicarage, taking{% endraw %}
{% raw %}part in the proceedings.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}New North Road Congregational Church, though capacious,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}was overcrowded on Thursday; and a second meeting in the large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}schoolroom was conducted at the same time, "Uncle Tom," after{% endraw %}
{% raw %}the chapel address, adjourning to supplement the other meeting,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to the intense delight of all.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Milton Road Congregational Church, in Stoke Newington, was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}also crowded in every part by a large company.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mayfield Terrace "Wesleyan Chapel on Sunday was, though large{% endraw %}
{% raw %}and commodious, filled to overflowing.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Mile End.—On Sunday, the great tent belonging to F. K".{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Charrington, Esq., was filled, "Uncle Tom" also delivering a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}lecture on the Monday evening.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The South Coast.—During the week "Uncle Tom" addressed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}large audiences in Portsmouth, Southampton, and in the Town{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Hall at Ryde, Isle of Wight.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Her Majesty's Ship "Victory."—"Uncle Tom" visited this{% endraw %}
{% raw %}famous vessel, which bore Nelson's flag, and on the deck of which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}he received his death-wound in the moment of triumph over the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}combined fleets of France and Spain, off Cape Trafalgar. By com¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}mand, the whole of the ship's officers and crew were collected to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}leceive an address from "Uncle Tom." To each of the company{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a copy was presented of the Christian Age, containing "Uncle{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Tom's " portrait.{% endraw %}
