{% raw %}METHODIST PREACHER.—VISIT FREE SOIL AND BEGIN MY{% endraw %}
{% raw %}STRUGGLE FOR FREEDOM.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}I arrived at Davis county, Kentucky, about the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}middle of April, 1825, and delivered myself and{% endraw %}
{% raw %}my companions to my owner's brother, Mr. Amos{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Riley, who had a large plantation with from eighty{% endraw %}
{% raw %}to one hundred negroes. His house was situated about{% endraw %}
{% raw %}five miles south of the Ohio River, and fifteen miles{% endraw %}
{% raw %}above the Yellow Banks, on Big Blackfords Creek.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}There I remained three years, and was employed{% endraw %}
{% raw %}meantime on the farm, of which I had the general{% endraw %}
{% raw %}management, in consequence of the recommendation{% endraw %}
{% raw %}for ability and honesty which I brought with me{% endraw %}
{% raw %}from Maryland. The situation was, in many re¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}spects, more comfortable than the one I had left.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}The farm was larger and more fertile, and there was{% endraw %}
{% raw %}a greater abundance of food, which is, of course,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}one of the principal sources of the comfort of a slave,{% endraw %}
{% raw %}debarred as he is from so many enjoyments which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}other men can obtain. Sufficiency of food is an im¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}portant item in any man's account of life ; it is{% endraw %}
