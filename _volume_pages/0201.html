{% raw %}MY FAMILY.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}A NE"W LIGHT IN MY DESOLATE HOME.—MY" CHILDREN.—MY THIRD{% endraw %}
{% raw %}VISIT TO ENGLAND.—MR. HUGHES.{% endraw %}
{% raw %}My heart and home were desolate after I lost the{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wife who had been my faithful companion in{% endraw %}
{% raw %}slavery, and had escaped with me to Canada. For{% endraw %}
{% raw %}four years it seemed to me her place could not be{% endraw %}
{% raw %}filled. I kept company with no one; I never{% endraw %}
{% raw %}walked out with any woman, and I thought it would{% endraw %}
{% raw %}be so to the end; but I was so lonely, so utterly{% endraw %}
{% raw %}miserable, that at last I decided that I would try to{% endraw %}
{% raw %}find another companion. I had travelled exten¬{% endraw %}
{% raw %}sively, and had made many acquaintances, but I{% endraw %}
{% raw %}knew of but one woman whom I cared to have for a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}wife. She was a widow, an estimable woman, one{% endraw %}
{% raw %}who had been a faithful teacher in the Sunday{% endraw %}
{% raw %}school, and quite a mother in the church to which{% endraw %}
{% raw %}she belonged. She had been brought up by a{% endraw %}
{% raw %}Quaker lady in Baltimore, and had received a good{% endraw %}
{% raw %}education in the ordinary branches. Her mother{% endraw %}
{% raw %}had been a slave, but was such a superior laundress,{% endraw %}
